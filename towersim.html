How to use the Tower Auction Simulator

What this tool does

This simulator models the auction dynamics of a trading card tower or repack. It runs Monte Carlo simulations to estimate total revenue across thousands of randomised reveal orders — showing you the range of possible outcomes, not just a single guess.

Cards are revealed one at a time. As each card is revealed, bidders recalculate the expected value (EV) of what remains. Big reveals create excitement; duds suppress subsequent bids. The order of reveals matters as much as the composition of the pool.

Step-by-step

  1. Build your card tiers. Add each tier (commons, rares, hits, etc.) with a value per card and quantity. The totals row updates live.
  2. Set tower settings. Enter your acquisition cost and target sale price. Total cards, retail value and average card value are auto-populated from your tiers.
  3. Add fees (optional). Add platform fees, payment processing costs, etc. Set each fee as a % or fixed $ amount, and choose whether it applies to the auction, the general sale, or both.
  4. Configure the auction model. Adjust bidder rationality, bid variance and excitement multiplier to match your audience. Defaults are reasonable starting points.
  5. Run the simulation. Click Run simulation. Results appear below with four views: revenue distribution, EV curve, run drill-down, auction vs sale comparison, and insights.
  6. Compare auction vs general sale. The Auction vs Sale tab shows what you'd earn if you sold every card individually at your target price, versus the simulated auction outcomes — both net of applicable fees.

Auction model parameters

Bidder rationality % — how close to EV a typical bidder pays. At 80%, a bidder seeing an EV of $1.00 will bid around $0.80. Lower for cautious audiences, raise for competitive regulars.

Bid variance % — random noise applied per bid to simulate human unpredictability. Higher values widen the distribution of outcomes. Set to 0 to disable.

Excitement multiplier — when a hit is revealed, subsequent bids are multiplied by this factor (capped at 2.5×). The effect decays at 0.95× per card reveal. Set to 1.0 to disable entirely.

2nd chance re-spin — after a card is revealed, the bidder can be offered a chance to discard it and draw again. This models a common live auction mechanic where the seller charges a second bid for the opportunity to swap.

2nd chance min bid — the gross bid must reach this threshold before a re-spin is offered. For example, set to $5.00 to only offer re-spins on cards where the bidder paid at least $5. Set to 0 to disable the 2nd chance mechanic entirely.

Re-spin threshold × — the bidder will want a re-spin if the revealed card is worth less than (average card value × this multiplier). At 1.0, they re-spin any card below average. At 0.5, they only re-spin cards worth less than half the average. Higher values mean bidders are harder to satisfy and re-spin more often.

Re-spin rationality % — how often the bidder actually follows the re-spin logic above versus acting randomly. At 100%, they always make the EV-correct decision. At 0%, they flip a coin. Bid variance adds additional noise on top. This only applies when a re-spin has been offered — it has no effect if 2nd chance min bid is set to 0.

Fees

Each fee can be a percentage (e.g. 5% platform commission applied to each sale) or a fixed amount (e.g. $0.30 per transaction). Set whether the fee applies to the auction, the general sale, or both. Fees are deducted from gross revenue when calculating net results.

Key metrics

Negative ROI rate — % of simulations where you lost money (revenue below acquisition cost).

10th percentile — your realistic worst-case: 90% of runs beat this number.

Value capture rate — revenue ÷ total retail card value. How much of retail value bidders actually paid.

Safe floor — the 10th percentile revenue. Use this as your conservative planning number.

Limitations

  • TBD how to exclude fixed tower fees for repeat buyers (e.g. shipping costs).
  • The model assumes all cards sell at auction. Real auctions may have unsold cards or passes.
  • Bidder behaviour is modelled as a simple rationality × EV formula. Real bidding involves social dynamics, platform-specific behaviour and collector psychology not captured here.
  • The excitement multiplier decays at a fixed rate — actual crowd excitement may spike or fade differently.
  • General sale comparison assumes 100% sell-through at the target price. Real sell-through rates vary.
  • Fees are applied uniformly per sale — tiered or subscription-based fee structures are not modelled.
